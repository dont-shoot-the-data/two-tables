# Six numbers, one program

**An investigative read of the Advance Peace Sacramento evaluation record.**

Live: [dont-shoot-the-data.github.io/two-tables](https://dont-shoot-the-data.github.io/two-tables/)

---

## TL;DR

Sacramento contracted with Advance Peace for a 20 to 25 percent reduction in gun-related assaults and homicides over two years. The program's own peer-reviewed evaluators have published at least six different reduction percentages for that window, ranging from 10.1 percent to 39 percent. Sacramento Police recorded 58 homicides in 2021, the highest count since 2006, while the program was still operating. The city declined to renew the contract in December 2021 and never publicly explained why.

This piece reads the program's own published record and traces the gap between what was promised, what was delivered, and what was claimed.

## Key findings

- The contract metric is documented in primary-source local news coverage of the December 2017 contract signing. The number is 20 to 25 percent. The unit is gun-related assaults and homicides combined, citywide, over two years.

- Sacramento PD homicide records show 36 in 2017, 35 in 2019, 41 in 2020, and 58 in 2021 (the highest single-year count since 2006). The 58-homicide year falls inside the program's operational window, not after it.

- The program's lead evaluator has published at least six different reduction percentages for the July 2018 to December 2019 intervention window in Sacramento, depending on the methodology, the geographic unit, and the venue. The 39 percent figure that anchored the UC Berkeley press release does not appear in the peer-reviewed paper that the press release was promoting.

- The program's flagship academic publication, in a Nature Portfolio journal, attributes the cause of urban gun violence to "structural violence... including dehumanizing policing, extreme poverty, and institutional racism," and on later pages of the same paper describes the operational mechanism as identifying African-American males under 24 and paying them up to one thousand dollars a month subject to mentor approval.

- The Center at Sierra Health Foundation is both an explicit racial-equity-framed nonprofit and the legal fiscal sponsor through which Advance Peace receives state CalVIP funding.

- Per Advance Peace COO Khaalid Muttaqi, in an August 2022 interview with The Appeal, five Sacramento fellows were arrested on gun-related charges during the 18-month fellowship and one fellow was shot and killed.

- Per The Trace, the New York City pilot announced March 2021 enrolled zero participants before Advance Peace withdrew in September 2022.

- Per CapRadio, four months after Sacramento ended the contract, the city manager's office had not publicly explained the reason for non-renewal.

## Methodology

Every numerical claim in the article traces to a primary document. Sources are listed at the end of the article with direct URLs. The Corburn 2022 Urban Science paper, the Corburn-Boggan-Muttaqi 2021 Humanities and Social Sciences Communications paper, and the Corburn-Boggan-Muttaqi 2022 Journal of Urban Health paper are all open access and can be downloaded from the publishers. Sacramento Police Department homicide statistics are drawn from the department's own year-end video releases and ABC10's reporting on those releases. Contract terms are sourced to the City of Sacramento's own news service, ABC10's December 2017 contract-signing coverage, and the Granicus archive for Resolution 2017-00851.

The article does not allege fraud. It documents inconsistencies in the published record and compares them to the contractual terms the City of Sacramento agreed to.
