# The metric the city paid for. The claim the city heard instead.

An investigation into the gap between what Sacramento contracted Advance Peace to deliver, what Advance Peace actually delivered, and what the program's allies told the public was delivered instead.

## What this is

In December 2017, the City of Sacramento signed Funding Agreement 2017-1473 with Advance Peace and Safe Passages. Section 5(b)(5) of the contract required, at minimum, a 20-25 percent reduction in citywide gun-related assaults and homicides during the program's first two years, and a 50 percent reduction at the end of any extended term.

Across the original two-year term (2018-2019), Sacramento homicides went from 36 in the 2017 baseline year to 36 in 2018 and 35 in 2019. Net change: roughly 1 percent. The contracted minimum was missed by approximately nineteen to twenty-four percentage points.

The City exercised its discretion to extend the program into 2020 and 2021. The extended-term standard required 50 percent reduction. Sacramento homicides in the extended term were 41 in 2020 and 58 in 2021. The 2021 number was the deadliest year Sacramento had recorded since 2006. The extended-term standard was missed by a margin running in the opposite direction: instead of falling 50 percent, citywide homicides rose more than 30 percent above baseline.

When the contract metric failed in the original term and was failing visibly in the extended term, the program's allies began circulating a different claim. The claim was that Sacramento had experienced "two consecutive years without a single juvenile homicide" under Advance Peace. The claim was repeated for five years by program staff, the program's UC Berkeley evaluators, the Sierra Health Foundation, the Giffords Law Center, the Sacramento Bee, ABC10, CapRadio, KCRA, the Sacramento Observer, the Sacramento News & Review, Newsweek, Jacobin, Ballotpedia, and a 2024 mayoral candidate who built her campaign around it and who is now an active candidate for Sacramento County Board of Supervisors District 1.

The Sacramento Police Department's own internal homicide records, obtained by the author through two separate California Public Records Act responses, contradict the claim. Case SA2018132511 was a 187(A) homicide that occurred at 13:21 hours on May 4, 2018, at 2380 Meadowview Road in South Sacramento, one of the program's three named target zones. Both SacPD responses confirm two juvenile victims were involved.

## What is still pending

Two SacPD California Public Records Act responses about case SA2018132511 contain different demographic information about the same case. The GOPerson dataset records the juvenile victims as ages 10 and 13. The May 1, 2026 narrative response identifies them as ages 3 and 6. The Department has not addressed or reconciled the discrepancy in either response.

Two further records requests are in progress as of publication:

1. A third California Public Records Act request to the Sacramento Police Department asking the Department to confirm which of its two prior responses about case SA2018132511 is correct.
2. A California Public Records Act request to the Sacramento County Coroner asking for independent confirmation of the names and ages of the children who died.

This article uses the framing that both SacPD responses agree on: two juvenile victims of a 187(A) homicide in case SA2018132511 on May 4, 2018, in a named Advance Peace target zone. The exact ages and names of the children are pending the Department's clarification and the Coroner's response. The age discrepancy itself, between two SacPD responses about the same case, is part of the institutional record this article documents.

## Methodology

Primary sources only. No editorializing. Each numerical claim cites a specific document. Each public retelling of the "no juvenile homicides" claim cites the specific publication and date where it appeared. The article does not assert that any institution in the chain of repetition consciously knew the claim was false. The article documents that none of the institutions verified the claim, and that the means to verify it were available to all of them through a routine California Public Records Act filing.
